# Bad End

## Core Concept

You play as the BBG (Big Bad Girl/Guy). The game presents itself as a standard action game — heroes storm your lair, you fight them off. Classic villain fantasy.

**The Twist:** The heroes aren't here to defeat you. They want to date you. The action game is actually a dating sim.

## Art Style

Western cartoon / indie comic style. Think Hazbin Hotel, Your Boyfriend, Crush On AI — thick linework, expressive faces, stylized proportions, dripping with personality. NOT anime. This style can be cute AND hot without committing to either. The BBG radiates "I'm the villain and I look GOOD doing it" energy. Stands out on a store page where every other dating sim is anime.

## Themes

**Consent** — baked into the core mechanics, not preachy. The dodge/accept system IS consent. You choose what you're okay with receiving. The game respects that choice mechanically. If you dodge, the hero doesn't force it. If you accept, they respond to your openness. Tanking everything isn't genuine — enthusiastic consent matters, not just "not saying no." This is taught through play, not dialogue.

**Consequences** — if you go too hard physically on a hero who is devoted to you, they come back next fight in a wheelchair, missing a limb. Still trying. Heartbreaking. You did that. The game doesn't lecture you — it just shows you what you did.

**Obedience vs. free thought** — the heroes were told you're evil. The "corruption" is just making people think for themselves. Crusades energy.

## Genre & Gameplay

The entire game plays out in an **Undertale-style battle view**. No overworld, no top-down exploration. Waves of heroes come to your lair, each one is a battle encounter. Between battles, a simple throne room VN scene acts as the hub — pick who to see next, read dialogue, then back into battle.

The game is **100% player skill**. Different heroes = different difficulty = different bullet patterns. The cocky knight has aggressive, fast patterns. The shy mage has sparse, wobbly ones. Skill expression IS character expression.

### The Heart

A tongue-in-cheek nod to Undertale. The player's avatar in the dodge arena is a heart — but it's not your soul. It's your feelings. In Undertale, your soul is at stake. In Bad End, your heart is.

### The Battle System IS The Dating System

The core mechanic is the bullet-hell dodge arena, but the meaning of dodging and getting hit shifts with context:

- **Dodging** = playing hard to get, rejecting their advance
- **Getting hit on purpose** = letting their flirting land, reciprocating, showing interest
- **Getting hit because you're flustered** = the BBG loses composure, movement speed drops because the hero said something smooth and your brain short-circuited
- **The hero fumbling** = their confidence is low, attacks become erratic, miss you entirely, projectiles literally apologize, the attack pattern tries to spell "I LIKE YOU" but it's all crooked and pathetic
- **The hero getting cocky** = they landed a hit (you liked what they said), now their attacks get bolder, faster, more elaborate

There is no separate dating UI. The battle system and the dating system are the same thing. That's the whole magic trick.

### Physical / Mental Split

Each battle has two dimensions:

**Physical turns** — traditional bullet-hell dodging. Swords, arrows, magic projectiles. These affect physical health. At ~50% physical health, a hero surrenders. At 0%, they die. You CAN kill, but the game remembers. A devoted hero you've brutalized shows up next fight broken — wheelchair, missing limb — still trying to reach you.

**Mental turns** — dialogue, emotional exchanges, telepathy (for heroes with that ability). These affect emotional state. Smooth lines make the BBG flustered (movement debuffs). The BBG's responses shift the hero's worldview on the conformity-vs-free-thought axis.

The two dimensions interact: "disarm and give hug" is a physical action with emotional consequences. Some heroes can probe your mind telepathically — you can guide them through your thoughts (specific hero gimmick, not universal).

### Player Skill vs. Dating Tension

The player naturally gets better at dodging over time — that's just muscle memory. But getting better at dodging means getting better at rejecting people. Your skill as a PLAYER works against you as the BBG trying to date. You have to fight your own muscle memory to let hits land. You have to intentionally be "worse" at the game to be "better" at dating.

But tanking everything isn't genuine either. The hero notices: "You're... not even trying to dodge? Do you even care or are you just saying yes to everyone?" The sweet spot is selective vulnerability — dodge some (keep your dignity), accept others (show genuine interest). The PATTERN of what you dodge and accept IS your dialogue.

### Defeated Mentally, Won Physically

A scenario: the hero got to you emotionally — you're flustered, caught feelings — but you still beat them in combat. They're unconscious on the floor and YOU'RE the one who's a mess. Do you let them go? Keep them? Tend to their wounds? This is a major character-defining moment that opens up exploration of consensual power dynamics — "I have power over you but I want you to WANT to be here."

### Ideological Tug of War

Each interaction shifts both characters on a spectrum:

**Hero pulling you toward "goodness"** = trying to make you surrender, conform, come back to society, accept the institution's rules. "Just come with me peacefully, they'll forgive you if you repent."

**You pulling the hero toward "evilness"** = making them question their orders, think independently, realize the institution that sent them isn't so righteous. "Forgive ME? For what exactly?"

The heroes are part of some institution — a church, a kingdom, an order — that TOLD them the BBG is evil. They never questioned it. They showed up with swords because someone in a fancy hat said to. Think crusades energy.

The "corruption" isn't turning good people evil — it's making obedient people think for themselves. The "goodness" pull isn't virtue — it's conformity.

The real bad guys aren't on the battlefield. They're the ones who sent the heroes in the first place.

## Structure

### Act 1 — The Flirt-Fight

Heroes storm your lair. You fight them in bullet-hell battles. The dating mechanics are already active — you're flirting while fighting. The player discovers the dual nature of the combat system organically.

Act 1 ends when you either land someone or someone lands you. This is a story split — who pursued who changes the power dynamic for the rest of the game.

### Act 2 — The Dating

You're with your chosen hero (or the one who chose you). They spend time with you, discover the truth: you're not just a baddie. All the "evil" you caused was self-defense. The institution lied about you. The hero starts questioning everything they were told.

### Act 3 — Confronting the Greater Evil

The two genres fuse. You and your love interest face the real threat — the institution that branded you evil and sent heroes to die at your door. Action and dating coexist. The game becomes what it always secretly was: both at once.

## The "Bad End"

The true bad ending: you dodge EVERYTHING. You reject every hero so completely they all give up and leave. Credits roll on an empty throne room. You won every fight. You're alone. That's a bad end.

## Open Questions

- **Tone:** Comedy with heart? Pure comedy? How deep does the institutional critique go?
- **The BBG:** Set character or player-defined? What's their backstory? Why did the institution target them?
- **Character depth:** How many dateable heroes? How deep are their arcs?
- **4th wall stuff:** Save it for one or two BIG moments? Window manipulation for the final confession? Save file awareness?
- **The split:** How different are the "you chose them" vs "they chose you" paths?
- **Gender:** Does the player's chosen gender matter? Is everyone bi/pan? Or go all-in on all-female cast (BBG and all heroes are women)?
- **Harem route:** Possible? Secret route? How does it interact with the "who pursued who" split?
- **Telepathy scope:** One hero's unique gimmick, or a broader mechanic?

## Ideas / Fragments

- Some heroes genuinely want to fight you. They resist the romance angle: "Your succubus tricks don't work on me!"
- Once you pick a love interest, the other heroes keep coming. Their friends are confused/disappointed: "Oh my god John, did you become evil too?? Just for some coochie?"
- The power dynamic is always in the BBG's favor — you're the one being courted, not the other way around.
- The name "Bad End" is a double meaning: in games, the bad ending is when the villain wins. In dating sims, "bad end" is a type of route outcome.
- Hero fumble mechanic: a nervous hero's attack pattern is a mess. Projectiles go the wrong way. They trip over their own sword. The "danger" becomes endearing.
- Flustered BBG mechanic: a hero lands a smooth line, your movement speed drops, your dodge window shrinks. You're not taking damage — you're catching feelings.
- Confident hero escalation: every "hit" you accept makes the hero bolder. Their patterns get more elaborate, more beautiful, more dangerous. They're showing off for you.
- A hero who falls for you hard enough might "defect" — their friends show up in Act 3 horrified that they switched sides.
- Wheelchair/amputee hero returning after being brutalized — still devoted, heartbreaking, teaches consequences through pure visual storytelling.
- The mental/flustered defeat scenario: BBG wins the fight but loses their composure. Opens up consensual captivity dynamics.
